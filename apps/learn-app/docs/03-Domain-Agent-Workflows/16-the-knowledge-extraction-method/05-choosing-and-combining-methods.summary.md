### Core Concept
The default rule for method selection is simple: Method A when knowledge lives in people, Method B when it lives in documents, both when it lives in both. The interesting question is not which method to use but how to handle the reconciliation when both apply — when expert judgement and documented standards give different answers for the same decision.

### Key Mental Models
- **Domain-Method Mapping**: Finance and sales are A-primary (the critical knowledge is tacit — calibrated professional judgement). HR and operations are B-primary (the critical knowledge is in documented policies and procedures). Legal, healthcare, and architecture are A+B (both substantial tacit and documented components).
- **Extraction Sequence**: A-primary domains start with the interview and verify against documents. B-primary domains start with document extraction and use the interview to resolve contradictions and fill gaps. A+B domains run both fully, starting with whichever is primary for the specific task.
- **The Reconciliation Principle**: Documented standards take precedence for regulatory compliance and professional liability. Expert judgement takes precedence for operational decisions within professional competence. The documented standard sets the boundaries; the expert judgement operates within them.

### Critical Patterns
- A-primary does not mean A-only — even finance and sales benefit from a targeted Pass One against documented standards to catch instructions the expert assumed or forgot
- B-primary does not mean B-only — the focused interview resolves contradictions and fills gaps that the documents create
- The reconciliation principle produces SKILL.md instructions that encode both sources: the documented constraint as a hard boundary and the expert judgement as operational guidance within it

### Common Mistakes
- Treating the domain-method mapping as rigid — specific organisations within a domain may differ based on documentation maturity
- Assuming expert judgement always trumps documented standards — in regulated domains, documented standards constrain expert judgement for compliance and liability
- Skipping Method B entirely in A-primary domains because "it is all about instinct" — documented standards still set boundaries the expert may have internalised
