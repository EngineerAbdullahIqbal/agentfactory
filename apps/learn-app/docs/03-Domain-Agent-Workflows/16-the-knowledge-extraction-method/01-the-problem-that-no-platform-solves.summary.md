### Core Concept
The knowledge that makes a domain agent genuinely useful is tacit — it resists articulation because it operates below conscious awareness. No platform improvement, model upgrade, or prompt engineering technique solves this without a structured extraction methodology. The articulation gap — the structural barrier between what an expert knows and what they can express in written instructions — is the fundamental problem the Knowledge Extraction Method exists to solve.

### Key Mental Models
- **Tacit vs Explicit Knowledge**: Explicit knowledge (frameworks, ratios, definitions) can be written down directly. Tacit knowledge (pattern recognition, calibrated judgement, exception handling) operates below conscious awareness and resists unstructured articulation. The tacit part is what makes a domain agent genuinely useful rather than merely generic.
- **The Articulation Gap**: Experts cannot write down what they know because the most valuable knowledge is not stored as articulable rules — it is embedded in pattern recognition developed through years of practice. Asking an expert to "write down what you know" produces the explicit half and omits the tacit half.
- **The Knowledge Extraction Method**: A two-mode structured process — Method A extracts from expert heads through interviews, Method B extracts from documents through a three-pass framework — designed to bridge the articulation gap.

### Critical Patterns
- A SKILL.md written without structured extraction encodes what the expert can articulate easily and omits the professional judgement that makes them valuable
- The credit analyst example illustrates the gap: the explicit knowledge is ratios and frameworks; the tacit knowledge is reading the gap between what financials say and what they imply
- Platform improvements make agents more capable at following instructions but do not solve the problem of what those instructions should contain

### Common Mistakes
- Assuming a domain expert can simply "write down" their expertise — this produces generic instructions that work for obvious cases and fail for the ones that matter
- Confusing model capability with extraction quality — a more capable model following vague instructions produces more confident-sounding but equally unreliable output
- Treating the explicit part of expertise (checklists, procedures) as the whole of expertise
